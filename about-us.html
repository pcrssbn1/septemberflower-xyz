The Significance of September Birth Flowers

Birth flowers have been associated with specific months for centuries, with roots in ancient Roman customs and Victorian-era flower language. The practice gained widespread popularity in the United States during the early 20th century, becoming as common as birthstones for representing one's birth month. September's assignment of the aster reflects both the flower's natural blooming period and its symbolic meanings that align with the characteristics of Virgo and Libra, the zodiac signs that span this month.

The aster's name comes from the ancient Greek word for 'star,' referring to its distinctive flower shape. According to mythology, asters were created when the goddess Astraea wept, and her tears fell to earth as stardust that became flowers. This mythological connection adds layers of meaning to the aster beyond its Victorian symbolism of love, wisdom, and faith. Native American tribes used asters medicinally and recognized their value in supporting late-season pollinators preparing for winter.

Morning glories complement asters as September's alternative birth flower, offering different but equally meaningful symbolism. The flower's daily cycle of opening at sunrise and closing by afternoon has inspired poets and philosophers for generations, representing the beauty of fleeting moments and the importance of seizing opportunities. In Japanese culture, morning glories (asagao) have been cultivated since the 8th century and symbolize summer's end and autumn's approach, perfectly aligning with September's transitional nature. Together, asters and morning glories provide September-born individuals with rich symbolic options that reflect both enduring wisdom and momentary beauty.
